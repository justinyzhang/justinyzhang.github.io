I am an economist-in-training working at the intersection of public and labor economics. This fall I begin my PhD in Economics at the University of Nebraska–Lincoln, supported by a Carpenter Fellowship. My research asks how the costs and benefits of policy travel across jurisdictional borders—who actually bears the burden when one state legalizes, taxes, or enforces while its neighbors do not. I work with administrative microdata and quasi-experimental designs, chiefly difference-in-differences and event studies.

Previously, I completed an MA in Economics, Data, and Policy at Central European University—with an exchange term at the Stockholm School of Economics—and a BSc in Economics at the University of Macau.

Working Papers

“Green Borders: Cross-Border Enforcement Spillovers from Cannabis Legalization”
Master’s Thesis · Central European University · Advisor: Sergey Lychagin · June 2026
[PDF]

Most studies of cannabis legalization focus only on the legalizing state, yet the enforcement burden can cross into neighbors that still prohibit it. Illinois opened a legal cannabis market in January 2020 while neighboring Iowa kept prohibition. Using a county–year difference-in-differences design with NIBRS offense counts (2015–2022), I compare the seven Iowa counties connected to Illinois by a direct highway bridge against the ninety-two interior counties. The event study does not reject parallel pre-trends. Recorded drug offenses in bridge counties rose by about 26% relative to the interior (β = 0.233, p = 0.024) and—more consequentially—recorded property offenses climbed about 37% (β = 0.312, p = 0.005), reaching a category that legalization does not directly cause. Violent offenses are positive but noisy, and driving offenses are flat. A cost–benefit account that counts only in-state tax revenue therefore understates the burden legalization places on its neighbor.
